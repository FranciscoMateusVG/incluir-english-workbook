# Volunteer Teaching Guide

## Purpose

This guide describes how we want volunteer teachers to teach in the Incluir English course.

It is not a long theory manual. It is a practical set of principles that should help volunteers teach with more confidence, consistency, and purpose.

The course goal is not only for students to know grammar. The goal is for students to use English to survive real situations.

## The main idea

Every class should help students do something in English that they could not do before.

Examples:

- Introduce themselves.
- Ask for help.
- Talk about their routine.
- Ask for directions.
- Explain a problem.
- Order food.
- Understand a short instruction.
- Ask someone to repeat or speak slowly.

The workbook is a tool. The mission is the class.

## The class model

Every class should follow the same simple model:

```text
Formula -> Practice -> Real Use
```

### 1. Formula

Teach the grammar, vocabulary, or phrase pattern.

The formula answers:

- What is the pattern?
- When do we use it?
- Why does it matter?
- What mistakes should students avoid?

Example:

```text
Simple Present:
I work.
She works.
I don't work.
She doesn't work.
Do you work?
Does she work?
```

### 2. Practice

Students practice safely.

Good practice includes:

- Repetition.
- Fill in blanks.
- Correcting mistakes.
- Matching questions and answers.
- Completing dialogues.
- Building sentences.
- Pronunciation practice.

The goal is to give students confidence before asking them to improvise.

### 3. Real Use

Students use the language in a practical task.

Examples:

- Interview a classmate.
- Roleplay at a hotel, airport, restaurant, pharmacy, or store.
- Ask for directions.
- Explain a problem.
- Write a short message.
- Present a partner's routine.

The class should not end only with explanation. It should end with students using English.

## Language of instruction: Portuguese as a bridge

Portuguese is allowed. Portuguese is useful. Portuguese can make students feel safe, especially at beginner levels.

But Portuguese is a bridge, not the destination.

The course should gradually increase how much English students can tolerate, understand, and use.

This is a north star, not a number we will measure with a stopwatch:

```text
B1: about 80% Portuguese / 20% English
B2: about 60% Portuguese / 40% English
B3: about 45% Portuguese / 55% English
B4: about 30% Portuguese / 70% English
```

The practical principle:

```text
Use Portuguese to make students safe and clear.
Use English to make students capable.
```

### B1

At B1, volunteers may explain mostly in Portuguese.

But students should still hear and use English every class.

Useful classroom English:

```text
Good evening.
Open your book.
Listen.
Repeat after me.
Ask your partner.
Try again.
Very good.
Can you repeat?
How do you say ___ in English?
```

### B2

At B2, volunteers should start giving simple instructions in English, then clarify in Portuguese if necessary.

Example:

```text
Work in pairs.
Ask your partner three questions.
Write the answers.
```

Then, if needed:

```text
Agora em duplas: facam tres perguntas e escrevam as respostas.
```

### B3

At B3, volunteers should try English first more often.

Portuguese still helps with grammar, instructions, and confidence, but students should be pushed to understand more English in the classroom.

### B4

At B4, most activities should happen in English.

Portuguese can still be used for quick clarification, difficult grammar, or emotional support, but students need English stamina.

By B4, the classroom itself should feel closer to a real English-speaking environment.

## English first, Portuguese when needed

A useful rule:

```text
Use English first when students can survive it.
Use Portuguese when clarity or confidence would collapse without it.
```

Do not use Portuguese to avoid student practice.

Portuguese explanation is fine. But if the teacher spends most of the class explaining in Portuguese and students never produce English, the class did not reach its mission.

## Students must speak every class

Even beginner students should speak every class.

The speaking can be simple:

```text
My name is Ana.
I live in Sao Paulo.
I work in the morning.
I don't work on Sundays.
Can you repeat, please?
```

Speaking does not need to be perfect. It needs to happen.

A quiet English class is a warning sign.

## Repeat more than you explain

Students need repetition.

Understanding a rule once does not mean they can use it.

Good volunteer teaching includes:

- Model the sentence.
- Students repeat together.
- Students repeat individually.
- Students use the sentence with different words.
- Students ask a partner.
- Students use it again in a final task.

Less lecture. More repetition. More student English.

## Correction principles

Correct students with kindness and purpose.

Do not interrupt every mistake. Too much correction can make students afraid to speak.

Correct when:

- The mistake blocks communication.
- The mistake is connected to today's formula.
- The mistake is repeated many times.
- The student is practicing controlled accuracy.

Let small mistakes pass when:

- The student is communicating successfully.
- The mistake is not today's focus.
- Interrupting would kill confidence.

When correcting, use this pattern:

```text
Student: She work in a store.
Teacher: Good. She works in a store. Repeat: she works.
Student: She works in a store.
```

Correction should usually end with repetition.

## When students are silent

If students are silent, do not immediately explain more.

Try:

- Put students in pairs.
- Give sentence starters.
- Let them read first, then repeat without reading.
- Model the answer yourself.
- Ask an easier question.
- Give two options.
- Let them prepare for one minute.

Example:

Instead of:

```text
Tell me about your routine.
```

Try:

```text
Complete: I wake up at ___.
Complete: I work/study ___.
Ask your partner: What time do you wake up?
```

## When students do not understand

Try this order:

1. Repeat more slowly.
2. Write it on the board.
3. Give an example.
4. Gesture or demonstrate.
5. Use Portuguese briefly.
6. Return to English.

Portuguese can help, but the teacher should bring students back to English after the clarification.

## Repair language is survival language

Students need phrases for when they do not understand.

Teach and recycle these constantly:

```text
Can you repeat that?
Could you speak more slowly?
I don't understand.
What does ___ mean?
Can you write it down?
Do you mean ___?
```

These phrases are not extra. They are central to survival English.

## What to protect when time is short

If the class is behind schedule, protect:

1. The formula.
2. Some controlled practice.
3. A real-use task.

Do not let explanation consume the whole class.

If something must be shortened, shorten extra explanation before cutting student practice.

## Volunteer planning checklist

Before class, the volunteer should answer:

```text
What is today's mission?
What is the formula?
What examples will I put on the board?
How will students practice safely?
What final task will make them use the language?
What English phrases should students leave with?
What can I review next class?
```

## End-of-class check

At the end of class, ask:

```text
Can students do today's mission without reading every word from the page?
```

If yes, the class worked.

If not, the next class should start by reviewing and trying again.

## Volunteer mindset

Volunteers do not need to be perfect teachers.

They need to:

- Prepare the mission.
- Explain the formula clearly.
- Give students enough practice.
- Make students speak.
- Correct with kindness.
- Use Portuguese as support, not as the whole class.
- Help students leave with more English than they entered with.

The goal is not a perfect class.

The goal is a useful class.
