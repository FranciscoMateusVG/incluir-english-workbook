# Assessment Model: Foundation Tests and Speaking Mission

## Purpose

If the course goal is survival English, assessment must measure more than grammar knowledge. Students should be evaluated on their ability to understand, write, read, use grammar and vocabulary, and eventually speak in realistic situations.

At the same time, the assessment system must be realistic for volunteer teachers. Testing speaking three times per semester may be logistically impossible.

The proposed model is:

```text
Test 1: Integrated Written/Listening Test   30%
Test 2: Integrated Written/Listening Test   30%
Test 3: Speaking Mission Exam               40%
```

In short:

```text
Two foundation tests + one performance test
```

## Core principle

The first two tests check whether students are building the foundation.

The final test checks whether students can use English to communicate.

Speaking is the most important single assessment, but the semester does not depend entirely on one stressful oral exam.

## Semester grade

```text
Final grade = (Test 1 x 0.30) + (Test 2 x 0.30) + (Test 3 x 0.40)
```

Example:

```text
Test 1: 72
Test 2: 80
Test 3: 70

Final grade:
(72 x 0.30) + (80 x 0.30) + (70 x 0.40)
= 21.6 + 24 + 28
= 73.6
```

## Test 1 and Test 2: Integrated Written/Listening Tests

Tests 1 and 2 are written/listening exams. They do not include individual speaking evaluation.

Each test should include:

- Listening.
- Reading.
- Writing.
- Grammar and vocabulary.

Suggested point distribution:

| Area | Points |
|---|---:|
| Listening | 25 |
| Reading | 20 |
| Writing | 20 |
| Grammar/Vocabulary | 35 |
| Total | 100 |

Grammar and vocabulary receive more weight in these tests because they are foundation exams. However, listening, reading, and writing must be protected so the exam does not become grammar-only.

## Why remove speaking from Tests 1 and 2

Speaking is essential, but individual speaking tests require time, trained evaluators, and a quiet environment. Doing this three times per semester may be too heavy for a volunteer-based course.

Instead, speaking should be practiced every class, but formally evaluated once in the final Speaking Mission Exam.

This keeps the system realistic without abandoning the communicative goal.

## Test 1 and Test 2 design

Tests should be organized around real-life situations, not random grammar lists.

Each test can have a theme.

Examples:

### B1

Test 1:
Personal information and classroom language.

Test 2:
Family, routine, and daily life.

### B2

Test 1:
Places, directions, food, and shopping.

Test 2:
Daily routines, quantities, descriptions, and present continuous.

### B3

Test 1:
Past events and personal experiences.

Test 2:
Future plans, advice, obligations, and comparisons.

### B4

Test 1:
Travel basics and personal plans.

Test 2:
Problems, services, customer support, health, and emergencies.

## Example structure for Test 1 or Test 2

### Listening: 25 points

Students listen to a short dialogue, announcement, message, or teacher-recorded audio.

Possible task types:

- Multiple choice.
- True/false.
- Fill in missing information.
- Match speakers to information.
- Answer short questions.

The audio should be short, clear, and connected to the course mission.

### Reading: 20 points

Students read a practical text.

Possible texts:

- A message.
- A sign.
- A schedule.
- A menu.
- A form.
- A receipt.
- A short profile.
- A simple email.

Possible task types:

- Find information.
- Match words to meanings.
- Answer short questions.
- Choose the best response.

### Writing: 20 points

Students write a short practical text.

Possible tasks:

- Introduce yourself.
- Write a short routine.
- Write a message asking for help.
- Write a complaint.
- Explain a problem.
- Describe plans.

Writing should be simple and communicative. The question should make clear what the student needs to include.

### Grammar/Vocabulary: 35 points

Students show they understand the formulas taught in class.

Possible task types:

- Fill in blanks.
- Correct mistakes.
- Transform sentences.
- Complete dialogues.
- Choose the correct option.
- Match vocabulary to situations.

Grammar should be tested in context whenever possible.

## Test 3: Speaking Mission Exam

The final test is a speaking-only performance exam.

This test asks:

Can the student complete realistic communication missions?

Suggested rubric:

| Area | Points |
|---|---:|
| Communication | 30 |
| Understands and responds | 20 |
| Target language | 20 |
| Pronunciation and clarity | 15 |
| Repair language | 15 |
| Total | 100 |

## Speaking rubric details

### Communication: 30 points

Can the student communicate the main message?

High score:

- Student completes the mission.
- Ideas are understandable.
- Student can continue even with mistakes.

Low score:

- Student cannot express the main idea.
- Student gives only isolated words.
- Student stops and cannot continue.

### Understands and responds: 20 points

Can the student understand the evaluator or partner and respond appropriately?

High score:

- Student answers questions.
- Student reacts to follow-up prompts.
- Student understands the basic situation.

Low score:

- Student does not understand basic questions.
- Student answers unrelated information.
- Student needs constant translation.

### Target language: 20 points

Can the student use the grammar, vocabulary, and phrases from the course?

High score:

- Student uses target structures correctly or mostly correctly.
- Student uses useful phrases from class.
- Student makes mistakes but communication remains clear.

Low score:

- Student avoids the target language completely.
- Errors prevent understanding.
- Student relies only on Portuguese or memorized fragments.

### Pronunciation and clarity: 15 points

Can the listener understand the student?

High score:

- Pronunciation is clear enough.
- Student speaks audibly.
- Pauses are natural or manageable.

Low score:

- Pronunciation frequently blocks understanding.
- Student speaks too quietly to evaluate.
- Student cannot produce enough English to assess.

### Repair language: 15 points

Can the student keep communication alive when they do not understand?

Useful repair language:

```text
Can you repeat that?
Could you speak more slowly?
I don't understand.
What does ___ mean?
Can you write it down?
Do you mean ___?
```

High score:

- Student asks for repetition or clarification.
- Student does not freeze immediately.
- Student tries to confirm understanding.

Low score:

- Student gives up when confused.
- Student cannot ask for help in English.
- Student switches immediately to Portuguese without trying repair language.

## Speaking exam logistics

To make the final exam possible with limited volunteers:

- Test students in pairs when possible.
- Use 5-7 minutes per pair.
- Score each student separately.
- Give students one or two short missions.
- While students wait, they complete a written review or reflection task.
- Use a simple printed rubric for evaluators.

Possible speaking formats:

### Pair roleplay

Student A and Student B receive roles.

Example:

- Student A is lost.
- Student B gives directions.

Then they switch roles or receive a second task.

### Evaluator roleplay

The evaluator plays the worker, receptionist, pharmacist, or airport employee.

The student completes the mission.

This gives more control, but requires more evaluator time.

### Station exam

Students rotate through short stations.

Examples:

- Directions station.
- Airport problem station.
- Pharmacy station.
- Hotel/customer service station.

This is strong pedagogically, but it requires more volunteers and planning.

## Example B4 Speaking Mission Exam

Student completes two of the following missions:

1. Airport: Your suitcase did not arrive. Explain the problem and ask what to do.
2. Pharmacy: You feel sick. Explain symptoms and ask for basic help.
3. Directions: You are lost. Ask how to get somewhere and confirm the route.
4. Hotel: There is a problem with your room. Explain the problem politely.
5. Customer service: Your card was declined or your order is wrong. Explain the situation.

The evaluator should listen for:

- Can the student explain the problem?
- Can the student answer follow-up questions?
- Can the student use target language?
- Can the student ask for repetition or clarification?
- Can the student stay in English long enough to complete the mission?

## Feedback profile

After each test, students should receive more than a grade.

Example:

```text
Student: Ana

Listening: 18/25
Reading: 16/20
Writing: 13/20
Grammar/Vocabulary: 28/35
Test total: 75/100

Feedback:
You understand the main idea well and your grammar is improving.
Your next focus is writing complete sentences and reviewing question forms.
```

For the Speaking Mission Exam:

```text
Student: Ana

Communication: 24/30
Understands/responds: 16/20
Target language: 14/20
Pronunciation/clarity: 12/15
Repair language: 8/15
Total: 74/100

Feedback:
You completed the mission and were understandable.
Your next focus is repair language: practice asking people to repeat or speak slowly.
```

This feedback helps students know what to study next.

## Final principle

The best assessment question is not:

Did the student memorize the grammar?

The best assessment question is:

Can the student complete the mission?

The assessment system should reward both foundation and performance.
