# Current vs Next-Semester Curriculum Map

## Purpose

This document compares what the current B1-B4 workbook teaches with what the next-semester course should prioritize.

The goal is not to throw away the current material. Much of the grammar and vocabulary is useful. The change is the organizing principle.

Current tendency:

```text
Teach grammar/topic -> practice grammar/topic -> maybe communicate at the end
```

Next-semester direction:

```text
Choose a real-life mission -> teach the formula needed -> practice -> use it
```

The new course should still teach grammar. But grammar should serve communication.

## Big progression

By the end of B4, students should be able to survive predictable situations in an English-speaking environment.

Suggested level identities:

| Level | New identity | Main student question |
|---|---|---|
| B1 | Personal and classroom survival | Can I talk about myself and function in class? |
| B2 | Daily life survival | Can I handle places, food, shopping, directions, and things happening now? |
| B3 | Stories, plans, advice, and problems | Can I talk about past/future situations and basic decisions? |
| B4 | Survival English capstone | Can I solve real-life problems in English? |

## B1: Personal and classroom survival

### What we have now

Current B1 chapters:

1. Day-to-Day Vocabulary and Verb To Be review.
2. Household Chores and Pronouns.
3. Plural and Simple Present.
4. Numbers Beyond 100.
5. Frequency Adverbs and Possessives.
6. Time and Time Prepositions.
7. People Descriptions, Transportation, and Directions.

Current strengths:

- Good foundation grammar.
- Useful vocabulary for daily life.
- Simple Present appears early.
- There are reading, writing, and interview activities.
- Students encounter routines, time, descriptions, transport, and directions.

Current risk:

- The level may feel like a sequence of grammar/vocabulary chapters instead of a survival path.
- Speaking may depend too much on the teacher choosing to activate the material.
- Classroom English and repair language need to become more explicit.

### What B1 should become

B1 should help students survive the classroom and introduce themselves in basic English.

Core missions:

1. Greet people and introduce yourself.
2. Spell your name and say contact information.
3. Ask the teacher to repeat, explain, or slow down.
4. Talk about family, work, school, and home.
5. Say what you do every day.
6. Tell the time and talk about your schedule.
7. Describe a person or place simply.
8. Ask and answer simple classroom/daily questions.

Grammar to keep:

- Verb to be.
- Subject and object pronouns.
- Possessive adjectives and pronouns.
- Plurals.
- Simple Present affirmative, negative, and questions.
- Frequency adverbs.
- Time expressions and prepositions.
- Basic WH questions.
- Basic prepositions of place.

Useful survival phrases:

```text
My name is ___.
I am from ___.
I live in ___.
I work/study ___.
I wake up at ___.
I don't understand.
Can you repeat, please?
How do you say ___ in English?
What does ___ mean?
```

Next-semester priority:

Make every B1 class end with students saying something about themselves or asking a partner something. B1 can use mostly Portuguese for explanation, but students should produce English every class.

## B2: Daily life survival

### What we have now

Current B2 chapters:

1. Present Simple and WH Questions review.
2. Plurality of Words and Countable/Uncountable Nouns.
3. Articles.
4. Quantifiers.
5. Quantifiers in Existential Sentences and Demonstratives.
6. Present Continuous.
7. Present Continuous expanded usage.
8. Connectors.
9. Connectors continued.
10. General Grammar Review.

Current strengths:

- Strong daily-life grammar base.
- Countable/uncountable nouns, quantifiers, and articles are useful for shopping and food.
- Present Continuous supports describing what is happening now.
- Connectors help students build longer sentences.
- There are readings and writing tasks.

Current risk:

- B2 may become too abstract: articles, quantifiers, connectors, and grammar review can feel disconnected from real life.
- Food, shopping, places, directions, and service interactions should be more central.
- Listening/speaking tasks need stronger presence.

### What B2 should become

B2 should help students handle daily-life situations.

Core missions:

1. Ask for and give basic directions.
2. Describe what is in a place.
3. Buy food or basic items.
4. Ask about prices and quantities.
5. Order something politely.
6. Describe what is happening now.
7. Talk about routine vs temporary situations.
8. Connect simple ideas with because, but, and, so.

Grammar to keep:

- Present Simple review.
- WH questions.
- Countable and uncountable nouns.
- A/an/the.
- Some, any, much, many, a lot of, a few, a little.
- There is / there are.
- This, that, these, those.
- Present Continuous.
- Present Simple vs Present Continuous.
- Basic connectors.

Useful survival phrases:

```text
How much is it?
I would like ___.
Do you have ___?
There is a ___ near here.
There are some ___.
I am looking for ___.
What are you doing?
I am waiting for ___.
I need some ___.
```

Next-semester priority:

Turn grammar topics into daily-life missions. Articles and quantifiers should appear inside shopping, food, home, classroom, and city tasks, not only as isolated forms.

## B3: Stories, plans, advice, and problems

### What we have now

Current B3 chapters:

1. Review of previous level content.
2. Modal Verbs: Advice, Obligation, and Prohibition.
3. Rights and Duties: Modal Verbs in Context.
4. Simple Future.
5. Simple Future expanded usage.
6. Simple Past introduction.
7. Simple Past practice and contextual usage.
8. Comparatives and Superlatives.
9. Intensifiers.

Current strengths:

- Modal verbs are very useful for advice, rules, obligations, and survival situations.
- Future forms support plans and predictions.
- Simple Past gives students the ability to explain what happened.
- Comparatives/superlatives support decision-making.
- The level has good potential for problem-solving tasks.

Current risk:

- B3 may still feel like grammar sequence: modals, future, past, comparatives.
- Simple Past should become a major communicative tool, not only a verb-form unit.
- Students need more practice explaining problems and asking for advice.

### What B3 should become

B3 should help students tell simple stories, explain past events, talk about plans, give advice, understand rules, and compare options.

Core missions:

1. Explain what happened yesterday or last week.
2. Tell a simple story in order.
3. Ask and answer questions about past events.
4. Talk about future plans.
5. Give advice and recommendations.
6. Explain rules, duties, and obligations.
7. Compare two options and choose one.
8. Describe a problem and ask what to do.

Grammar to keep:

- Should, must, have to, can, could.
- Future with will and going to.
- Present Continuous for future arrangements.
- First conditional.
- Simple Past regular and irregular verbs.
- Was/were and there was/there were.
- Past questions and negatives.
- Comparatives and superlatives.
- Too/enough and basic intensifiers.

Useful survival phrases:

```text
What happened?
I lost ___.
I missed ___.
I went to ___.
I didn't understand.
What should I do?
You should ___.
You have to ___.
I am going to ___.
Which one is better?
This is cheaper/more expensive.
```

Next-semester priority:

Make B3 the bridge from sentence-level English to problem-solving English. Students should leave B3 able to explain a simple past problem and ask for advice.

## B4: Survival English capstone

### What we have now

Current B4 chapters:

1. Tenses Review: Present and Past.
2. Future Simple, Modal Verbs, and Prepositions.
3. Present Perfect and Past Participle.
4. Present Perfect continued and Reported Speech.
5. Past Perfect.
6. Passive Voice.
7. Relative Clauses and For vs To.
8. Phrasal Verbs and Collocations.

Current strengths:

- Strong grammar consolidation.
- Present Perfect, Passive Voice, modals, and phrasal verbs can be very useful in real-life contexts.
- There are readings, review worksheets, and a B4 test.
- B4 is the most developed current level.

Current risk:

- B4 is the level most at risk of becoming grammar-heavy.
- Some grammar, like Past Perfect and Reported Speech, may be over-weighted compared with speaking/listening survival.
- Students may finish B4 knowing advanced forms on paper but still struggle to speak or understand real interactions.

### What B4 should become

B4 should be the survival English capstone.

Core missions:

1. Introduce yourself in a new environment.
2. Navigate airport, bus, train, or arrival situations.
3. Ask for and confirm directions.
4. Check in at a hotel or explain a room problem.
5. Order food, mention allergies, and handle a wrong order.
6. Explain symptoms at a pharmacy or clinic.
7. Report a lost/stolen item.
8. Handle payment, refund, or customer service problems.
9. Ask for clarification at work or volunteering.
10. Complete a final "day abroad" speaking mission.

Grammar to keep:

- Present/Past/Future review.
- Modals for requests, advice, obligation, and possibility.
- Prepositions of time/place.
- Present Perfect for experiences and recent problems.
- Passive Voice for problems and services.
- Relative clauses for identifying people/places/things.
- Phrasal verbs for everyday actions.
- Collocations for natural communication.

Grammar to reduce or reframe:

- Past Perfect should be taught as useful for sequencing stories, not as a major isolated unit.
- Reported Speech should be reduced or used only when it supports real tasks like explaining what someone told you.
- Advanced grammar should not displace listening, speaking, and roleplay.

Useful survival phrases:

```text
I have lost my passport.
My bag was stolen.
My card was declined.
I was told to come here.
I need to change my reservation.
I have a headache.
Do I need a prescription?
Could you repeat that?
Could you speak more slowly?
What should I do now?
```

Next-semester priority:

Turn B4 into a performance level. Every unit should prepare students for a realistic survival mission, and the final assessment should be a Speaking Mission Exam.

## Suggested next-semester structure

This is a first draft. It should guide planning, not freeze the curriculum.

### B1 suggested units

1. Classroom English and introductions.
2. Personal information and verb to be.
3. Family, work, and basic pronouns.
4. Daily routine and Simple Present.
5. Time, days, and schedules.
6. Places, classroom objects, and prepositions.
7. Describing people and things.
8. Review mission: introduce yourself and ask basic questions.

### B2 suggested units

1. Daily routine review and WH questions.
2. Food, shopping, and countable/uncountable nouns.
3. Prices, quantities, and quantifiers.
4. Places, there is/there are, and demonstratives.
5. Directions and transportation.
6. Present Continuous: what is happening now.
7. Routine vs now vs near future.
8. Review mission: daily-life problem-solving.

### B3 suggested units

1. Review: routines, directions, and daily life.
2. Rules, advice, and obligations with modals.
3. Future plans and arrangements.
4. Simple Past: what happened?
5. Past questions and storytelling.
6. Problems and advice.
7. Comparisons and decisions.
8. Review mission: explain a problem and choose a solution.

### B4 suggested units

1. Arrival and introductions in a new place.
2. Airport, transport, and directions.
3. Hotel, housing, and room problems.
4. Food, shopping, payments, and wrong orders.
5. Health, pharmacy, and emergencies.
6. Lost items, customer service, and complaints.
7. Work/volunteering instructions and clarification.
8. Final survival simulation: a day abroad.

## Cross-level non-negotiables

Every level should include:

- Formula -> Practice -> Real Use.
- Speaking every class.
- Listening or teacher-modeled input every class.
- Repair language every level.
- AI study mission or offline alternative.
- Two foundation tests and one speaking mission exam.
- Gradual increase in English used by the teacher.

Language-of-instruction north star:

```text
B1: about 80% Portuguese / 20% English
B2: about 60% Portuguese / 40% English
B3: about 45% Portuguese / 55% English
B4: about 30% Portuguese / 70% English
```

## What should not change

The course should not abandon grammar.

The current grammar sequence contains many useful formulas. The change is that each formula should be connected to a mission.

Examples:

| Grammar | Mission |
|---|---|
| Verb to be | Introduce yourself and describe people. |
| Simple Present | Talk about routines. |
| Quantifiers | Buy food and describe what you need. |
| Present Continuous | Describe what is happening now. |
| Modals | Ask for help, give advice, explain rules. |
| Simple Past | Explain what happened. |
| Future forms | Talk about plans. |
| Passive Voice | Report problems: `My bag was lost.` |
| Relative Clauses | Identify places/things: `It is the place where I am staying.` |
| Phrasal verbs | Understand everyday actions: `check in`, `fill out`, `get off`. |

## Next planning steps

1. Approve or adjust the level identities.
2. Choose the exact next-semester unit map for each level.
3. Create a standard lesson template for all levels.
4. Rewrite one pilot unit using the new model.
5. Train volunteers using the Volunteer Teaching Guide.
6. Build Test 1, Test 2, and Speaking Mission models for each level.
7. Gradually revise the workbook content.

The next-semester curriculum should be judged by one question:

Can students do more English with their own voices than they could before?
