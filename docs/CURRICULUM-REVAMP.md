# Curriculum Revamp: From Grammar Coverage to Survival English

## Working thesis

The next version of the Incluir English course should be designed around what students can do with English, not only around which grammar points they have seen.

By the end of B4, a student who started in B1 should be able to survive in an English-speaking environment: ask for help, understand essential information, explain basic problems, handle common services, recover when they do not understand, and participate in simple social, study, travel, and work situations.

This does not mean grammar disappears. Grammar becomes support for action.

Instead of asking, "Did we teach the passive voice?", the course should ask, "Can the student say and understand things like `My card was declined`, `My bag was stolen`, `I was told to come here`, and `Could you help me?`"

## Why change

The current material is strong as a workbook production system, but the course risks becoming too grammar-led. A student can reach B4 having studied many structures and still struggle to talk, listen, or solve a basic real-world problem in English.

That is a curriculum design problem, not a student problem.

If the final level is mostly advanced grammar practice, it may reward students who can identify forms on paper while leaving them unprepared for actual communication. The course needs a clearer performance target: English as a tool for everyday survival.

## Research basis

Modern language-teaching frameworks support this shift.

The CEFR describes language learners as social agents who use language to complete tasks in real contexts. Its Companion Volume frames the action-oriented approach as a move away from syllabuses based mainly on a linear progression through language structures and toward syllabuses based on needs analysis, real-life tasks, and "Can do" descriptors.

ACTFL's Can-Do Statements serve a similar purpose: they help teachers write learning targets around what learners can communicate, and help learners understand their own progress through practical communicative goals.

Task-based and communicative language teaching also point in this direction. The core idea is that students need meaningful practice doing things with language, not only controlled practice about language.

- ACTFL Can-Do Statements: https://www.actfl.org/educator-resources/ncssfl-actfl-can-do-statements

## New course principle

Teach for performance, not coverage.

Coverage asks:

- Did we teach present perfect?
- Did we teach modal verbs?
- Did we teach passive voice?
- Did we teach relative clauses?

Performance asks:

- Can the student explain what happened?
- Can the student ask for clarification?
- Can the student understand a short instruction?
- Can the student describe a problem?
- Can the student make a request politely?
- Can the student survive a predictable interaction without switching immediately to Portuguese?

## Proposed B1-B4 progression

The grammar sequence can remain visible in the background, but the student-facing and teacher-facing spine should be communicative.

### B1: Personal and classroom survival

Students learn to introduce themselves, talk about personal information, understand classroom language, ask basic questions, describe people and things, and handle simple routines.

Example outcomes:

- I can introduce myself and ask someone's name.
- I can say where I am from and ask where someone is from.
- I can ask the teacher to repeat or explain.
- I can talk about family, work, school, and simple daily objects.

### B2: Daily life survival

Students learn to talk about routines, places, food, shopping, quantities, locations, directions, and things happening now.

Example outcomes:

- I can describe my routine.
- I can ask where something is.
- I can buy food or ask about prices.
- I can describe what is happening now.
- I can understand simple public or classroom instructions.

### B3: Plans, obligations, and past experiences

Students learn to discuss past events, future plans, obligations, advice, rules, comparisons, and basic problems.

Example outcomes:

- I can explain what happened yesterday or last week.
- I can talk about future plans.
- I can say what I have to, need to, or should do.
- I can compare options.
- I can ask for and give basic advice.

### B4: Survival English capstone

B4 should become the final practical integration level. It should recycle grammar and vocabulary through realistic tasks that prepare students for an English-speaking environment.

Example outcomes:

- I can get through an airport or bus/train station interaction.
- I can ask for directions and confirm that I understood.
- I can check in at a hotel or explain a housing problem.
- I can order food, mention allergies, and handle a wrong order.
- I can explain symptoms at a pharmacy or clinic.
- I can understand basic work or volunteering instructions.
- I can deal with a payment problem, refund, lost item, or customer service situation.
- I can ask someone to repeat, slow down, write something down, or explain a word.

## What B4 could become

B4 should be less "advanced grammar level" and more "survival fluency capstone."

Possible unit map:

1. Airport and arrival
2. Directions and transportation
3. Housing, hotels, and everyday problems
4. Food, shopping, prices, and allergies
5. Health, pharmacy, and emergencies
6. Work, volunteering, and instructions
7. Customer service, complaints, and digital life
8. Final simulation: a day abroad

Each unit should still include grammar, but grammar should be attached to the situation.

Examples:

- Present perfect: `I have lost my passport.`
- Past simple: `I missed my bus.`
- Modals: `Could you help me?` / `I need to see a doctor.`
- Future: `I am going to stay for two weeks.`
- Passive voice: `My card was declined.`
- Relative clauses: `It is the place where I am staying.`
- Phrasal verbs: `fill out`, `check in`, `get off`, `pick up`, `look for`.

## Lesson design non-negotiables

Every class should include:

- Listening practice.
- Speaking practice.
- Repair language practice.
- One real-world communicative task.
- A short review of previous language.
- Some controlled grammar/vocabulary practice, but not as the whole lesson.

Repair language should be practiced constantly:

- Sorry, can you repeat that?
- Could you speak more slowly?
- What does ___ mean?
- Could you write that down?
- I do not understand the last part.
- Do you mean ___?

This is survival language. It lets students keep communicating even when their English is incomplete.

## Workbook implications

The workbook should support the course, not replace the course.

Each unit should include:

- A real-world situation.
- A short model dialogue.
- Key survival phrases.
- Listening tasks or listening scripts.
- Controlled practice for accuracy.
- Pair speaking tasks.
- A final performance task.
- Teacher notes with suggested timing and classroom flow.
- A short Can-Do checklist.

The existing HTML-to-PDF system can support this well. The main change is not technical; it is curricular.

## Assessment implications

Assessment should include performance, not only written grammar.

Possible B4 final assessment:

- Listening: understand short real-world announcements or dialogues.
- Speaking: complete roleplay stations.
- Reading: understand signs, forms, menus, messages, or simple instructions.
- Writing: write a short message asking for help or explaining a problem.
- Grammar: use target structures accurately enough to communicate.

The final simulation could include stations such as:

- Airport immigration question.
- Asking for directions.
- Pharmacy symptom explanation.
- Restaurant order problem.
- Hotel check-in or room problem.
- Lost phone or lost bag report.
- Work/volunteer instruction clarification.

## Next steps

1. Define the final B4 graduate profile in concrete Can-Do statements.
2. Map B1-B4 around communicative outcomes first, grammar second.
3. Redesign one B4 unit as a pilot.
4. Test that unit with students and teachers.
5. Use the pilot to create the course template.
6. Gradually revise the workbook content to follow the new model.

The goal is not to make the course less rigorous. The goal is to make the rigor visible in real communication.
