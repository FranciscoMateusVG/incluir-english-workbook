# Class Model: Formula, Practice, Real Use

## Purpose

Incluir English classes are usually two hours long and are often taught by volunteers. The class model must be simple enough to remember, flexible enough for different teachers, and strong enough to make students actually use English.

The proposed model is:

1. Formula
2. Practice
3. Real Use

Grammar remains important. It gives students the formula. But students only understand and remember the formula when they practice it and use it to communicate.

## Core belief

Grammar is the formula. Practice is where students understand it. Communication is where they prove they can use it.

The course should avoid two extremes:

- Grammar-only classes, where students complete exercises but cannot speak or listen.
- Conversation-only classes, where students speak but repeat the same mistakes because nobody gave them the structure.

The course needs the middle path:

Teach the formula clearly. Practice it safely. Use it in a real situation.

## Two-hour class structure

The timing is approximate. Volunteers should not feel trapped by the clock, but every class should move through the three stages.

| Time | Stage | Purpose |
|---:|---|---|
| 0-35 min | Formula | Explain the grammar, vocabulary, or phrase pattern. Show why it matters. |
| 35-80 min | Practice | Students do controlled practice with correction and repetition. |
| 80-120 min | Real Use | Students use the language in a speaking, listening, writing, or roleplay task. |

This can also be described as:

- Learn the pattern.
- Train the pattern.
- Use the pattern.

## Stage 1: Formula

The teacher presents the key structure for the day.

This stage should answer:

- What is the pattern?
- When do we use it?
- What are common mistakes?
- Why does this matter outside the classroom?

The explanation should be short, concrete, and connected to a real situation.

Example:

Passive voice is useful when the action is more important than the person who did it.

- Active: `Someone stole my bag.`
- Passive: `My bag was stolen.`
- Formula: `object + be + past participle`
- Real situation: airport, police station, hotel, customer service.

## Stage 2: Practice

Students practice the structure safely before they have to improvise.

Useful practice types:

- Repeat key sentences.
- Fill in blanks.
- Match questions and answers.
- Choose the correct option.
- Correct common mistakes.
- Transform sentences.
- Complete short dialogues.
- Practice pronunciation of key phrases.

The goal is accuracy and confidence.

Students should hear and say the target language many times before the final task.

## Stage 3: Real Use

Students use the language to complete a practical task.

This stage should feel like a mission:

- Ask for directions.
- Explain a health problem.
- Check in at a hotel.
- Report a lost item.
- Order food and solve a problem.
- Ask someone to repeat or slow down.
- Understand and respond to basic instructions.

The task does not need to be complicated. It needs to force students to use the formula for a real communicative purpose.

Example:

You are at the airport. Your suitcase did not arrive. Report the problem.

Students should be able to say things like:

- My bag was lost.
- It was checked in at Sao Paulo.
- I was told to come here.
- What should I do now?

## Example: B4 passive voice class

### Formula

Passive voice:

`object + be + past participle`

Use it when the action or result matters more than the person who did the action.

Examples:

- My card was declined.
- My bag was stolen.
- The form must be completed.
- The reservation has been canceled.

### Practice

Students:

- Choose `is`, `was`, `were`, or `has been`.
- Transform active sentences into passive sentences.
- Correct mistakes like `My phone was steal`.
- Complete a short customer service dialogue.

### Real Use

Roleplay:

Student A is at an airport help desk. Student B works there. Student A explains that their bag was lost and asks what to do.

The teacher listens for:

- Correct passive structure.
- Clear pronunciation.
- Use of repair language.
- Ability to continue even with imperfect grammar.

## Example: B4 directions class

### Formula

Asking for and giving directions:

- How do I get to ___?
- Is it far from here?
- Go straight.
- Turn left/right.
- It is next to ___.
- Could you repeat that?

### Practice

Students:

- Match places and directions.
- Listen to a short dialogue.
- Fill in missing direction phrases.
- Practice pronunciation and intonation.

### Real Use

Roleplay:

One student is lost in a new city. The other gives directions. Then they switch roles.

The teacher listens for:

- Can the student ask for help?
- Can the student understand the route?
- Can the student ask for repetition?
- Can the student confirm understanding?

## Non-negotiables

Every two-hour class should include:

- A clear formula.
- Controlled practice.
- Real use.
- Some speaking.
- Some listening or teacher-modeled input.
- Repair language.

Repair language should appear constantly across the course:

- Sorry, can you repeat that?
- Could you speak more slowly?
- What does ___ mean?
- Could you write that down?
- I do not understand the last part.
- Do you mean ___?

These phrases help students survive when their English is incomplete.

## Teacher mindset

The teacher does not need to explain everything perfectly. The teacher needs to move students from understanding the formula to using it.

The best question at the end of class is:

Can students do today's mission without reading every word from the page?

If yes, the class worked.
